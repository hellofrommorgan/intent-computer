The Intent Loop

The decision engine underneath. Every turn, your agent reads your context, checks what matters, and decides what to do — before you ask.

Five layers, every turn

Perception What's happening? Read the state of your knowledge. Spot problems: unprocessed inbox items, disconnected notes, stale commitments.
↓
Identity Who am I and what do I care about? Load your agent's goals, working memory, and sense of what matters.
↓
Commitment Which problems matter? Compare what's wrong against what you care about. Some things need attention now; some can wait.
↓
Memory What do I already know about this? Load the relevant parts of your context graph — not everything, just what's needed.
↓
Execution What should I do? Propose actions, check permissions, execute or suggest. Record what happened.

This runs on every session start and whenever your agent detects a shift in what you're working on. It's how your agent stays oriented instead of just reacting to your last message.

Perception

The perception layer reads the state of your knowledge and surfaces signals (observations) and gaps (problems). Is your inbox piling up? Are there notes with no connections? Have you been ignoring a commitment for two weeks?

Perception is threshold-gated — small vaults don't trigger noise, but real issues at scale get caught. Three unprocessed items in the inbox triggers attention. Five disconnected notes triggers connection work.

Identity

Your agent maintains a sense of who it is and what you care about, drawn from three sources:

  • Identity — the agent's voice, role, and relationship to you
  • Goals — your active commitments with priority and timeframe
  • Working memory — what happened recently, updated every session

This layer also detects drift — when what you're actually doing diverges from what you said matters. It won't stop you, but it will notice.

Commitment

This is where prioritization happens. The loop takes the problems perception found and the goals identity loaded, and asks: which of these actually matter?

Some problems are urgent — broken links, overflowing inbox. Some can wait — a few disconnected notes aren't an emergency. The commitment layer sorts them so your agent focuses on what's important, not just what's recent.

Memory

The memory layer loads the parts of your context graph that are relevant to your current commitments. Not the whole graph — just the subset that matters right now. This is how session 50 knows everything session 1 learned, without drowning in irrelevant context.

Execution

The execution layer proposes actions and checks whether it has permission to take them:

  • Autonomous — just does it (e.g., auto-committing after a write)
  • Delegated — does it with your approval
  • Advisory — suggests but doesn't act
  • Disabled — won't even suggest it

You control exactly how much autonomy your agent has. Every action, every level — configured in a single policy file.

The heartbeat

Between sessions, a background scheduler runs every 15 minutes. It checks your commitments, picks up queued tasks that align with your goals, detects stale work, and writes summaries. Your agent keeps tending the garden even when you're not looking.

The heartbeat doesn't do full reasoning — it handles maintenance and surfaces what needs attention. Deep thinking stays in-session where you can see it.

The architecture

interface IntentComputerLayers { perception: PerceptionPort; // what's happening? identity: IdentityPort; // who am I? commitment: CommitmentPort; // what matters? memory: MemoryPort; // what do I know? execution: ExecutionPort; // what should I do? }

Five layers. Each one is a clean interface with a swappable implementation. Today they read your local filesystem. Tomorrow they could read a cloud API. The loop stays the same.
